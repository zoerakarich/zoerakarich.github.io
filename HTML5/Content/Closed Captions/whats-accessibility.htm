What is Accessibility?

Types of Disabilities

There is a wide spectrum of disabilities that can be hereditary, acquired, visible or invisible.

Sensory disabilities relate to one of our senses whether it be seeing, hearing or feeling. These disabilities can people significantly or minorly. For example, some may be blind while others are colorblind, which affect their vision very differently.

Physical disabilities affect a person’s mobility, dexterity or stamina and include motor disabilities, paralysis, amputation and deformity. Those with physical disabilities may have trouble moving or controlling muscle groups necessary for a certain function.

Intellectual, developmental, neurological and psychological disabilities impact a person’s brain or nervous system and can also include sensory or physical symptoms. Some of these disabilities may be invisible to the naked eye but affect a person’s perception and life experience remarkably.

Approaches to Accessibility

Accessible design considers the needs of those with disabilities, ensuring they have some form of access. When the needs of disabled people are considered after something is already constructed, there is a greater chance that the accommodation is more inconvenient than it would have been if accessibility was considered from the get-go.

As an evolution of accessible design, universal design takes accessibility a step farther by considering the widest, most diverse possible audience. When initially the planning stage, the needs of all people are considered from the get-go. This typically results in the most efficient accommodations which are often more integrated.

Accessible design and universal design can be applied to physical and digital spaces. Many disabled people use assistance online to help them process information in the way that best suits them.

The Importance of Digital Accessibility

Just as people require accommodations in real life, many require them to properly process information on the web. Equal access to information is denied if a web page is inaccessible.

Assistive technology is software or hardware often used to benefit disabled users and their needs. Screen readers, screen magnifiers, eye trackers, switch devices, and on-screen keyboards are common tools utilized online.

Without the help of assistive technologies, some people would not otherwise be able to engage with digital content. For instance, hearing impaired people use transcripts or subtitles and visually impaired people use audio descriptions to get the full picture.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requires public-facing state and local governments and businesses to make their websites accessible to disabled individuals. ADA also authorizes the Department of Justice to provide technical assistance to those with rights or responsibilities under the act.

Website Accessibility Barriers

Unfortunately, certain digital barricades happen frequently, hindering users.

Misusing color can drastically affect a visually impaired individual’s interpretation of content. A lack of color contrast between the text and background can prove difficult for colorblind people or those with limited vision to see. Using color alone to distinguish information also inhibits some folks who may not be able to tell the shade difference.

In terms of context, both imagery and video need supplemental information to make sure every user has sufficient access. “Alt text,” an abbreviated form of text alternatives, is used by blind individuals to understand the purpose and relevance of an image in relation to the web page. And those with hearing disabilities utilize captions on video to comprehend the audio.

Some folks are unable to navigate the web without a keyboard. Mouse-only navigation prevents them from using a trackpad, thus excluding them from interacting with the site.

Making Content Accessible

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are requirements addressing problems that disabled users’ experience. Web content developers, web authoring tool developers and web accessibility evaluation tool developers use WCAG as a technical standard to ensure that disabled people can properly access what they want to online.

WCAG is organized into four principles: perceivable, operable, understandable and robust. These principles can be remembered using the acronym ‘POUR.’ Content, interface components, and navigation must be able to be perceived, operate correctly, be understood, and considered robust enough to be interpreted by a variety of user agents, including assistive technologies.
